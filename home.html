Commitment for Life

How does faith guide you in finding your perfect match?

Finding a perfect match is often seen as a journey of love, trust, and divine guidance. From a theological perspective, faith plays a central role in this process, shaping our understanding of relationships, commitmment, and purpose. Here are ways in which faith can guide you:

  • Trusting in Divine Timing.
  • Seeking Compability in Faith and Values.
  • Love as a Reflection of Divine Love.
  • Discernment Through Prayer and Community.
  • Understanding Marriage as a Sacred Covenant.

Thus, faith teaches that finding a perfect match is not about searching for an idealized person but about becoming the right person, someone who embodies love, patience, and faith. It helps individuals not only find a partner but also build a relationship that reflects the divine plan.

The Institution of Marriage

The institution of marriage, as we know it today in most of the known world, is the product of the evolution and convergence of three cultural traditions:

  • Jewish Tradition
  • Roman Tradition
  • Germanic Tradition

Jewish Tradition

In Jewish Tradition, marriage is contained in the Gensis creation accounts and insists on the twofold meaning of marriage, procreation and union, though procreation eventually took priority.

In addition, the well-being of children is highly valued. They play a crucial role in ensuring survival, growth, and resilience of the chosen people. As a result, having many children is seen as a profound blessing from the Lord. Marriage was primarily for bearing children to:

  • Preserve the husband's family
  • Maintain the existence of the Jewish people
  • Fullfill Israel's covenant with God

2 stages of Jewish Marriage

  • Kiddushin (Betrothal)
  • Nissuin (Wedding proper

Kiddushin or Solemn Betrothal

Though Technically an engagement, was legally binding and breakable only by divorce and required sexual fidelity.

Nissuin or Wedding Proper

A year later kiddushin, the nissuin involved a week-long family feast led by the groom's father, not religious figures. The ceremony ended when the bride's father "gave her away" and with the groom leading his bride to his (or his parents') home, marking the finalization of marriage.

Roman Tradition

In Roman Tradition, requires (1) the capacity of the man and of the woman to marry; (2) their desire to marry; (3) the expression of this desire.

Roman marriages evolved through three stages:

  • Confarreatio - An early religious ceremony involving a sacrifice to jupiter
  • Coemptio - A symbolic "purchase" of the bride, transferring her from father's power to her husband's
  • Matrimonium Liberium - By the 2nd and 3rd centuries B.C.E., this became the norm in treating women as equals, allowing them to keep their name, share their home equally, and even initiate divorce

Matrimonium Clandestinum

Later, Roman influence led to matrimonium clandestinum (Secret Marriage, where mutual consent alone created a valid marriage — no witnesses or ceremony needed. This cause problems, like spouses denying the marriage later, with no way to prove the truth.

Church ended Matrimonium Clandestinum and the Council of Trent (1545-1563) Tametsi Decree (1563)

With this decree, a canonical form was required for a valid marraige, established:

  • Marriage must be witnessed by a priest and two others
  • Public announcements (banns) on three Sundays before marriage
  • Secret marriages declared invalid.

Germanic Tradition

The Germanic people found fault with the Roman way of marrying, which focused on the exchange of marital consent.

They insisted on a series of acts:

  • Betrothal or Engagement - the mutual exchange of a promise to marry
  • Desponsatio - the exchange of the wedding pledges, with the groom giving the bride a ring and arrhae and the wife giving the groom her dowry
  • Wedding proper - the mutual exchange of marital consent
  • Consummation - the first sexual intercourse of the couple after the wedding

St. Augustine defended marriage by highlighting three key "goods of marriage" (bona matrimonii), or the goods of marriage. He classified thse good into two groups: (a) primary goods and (b) secondary goods.

Primary Goods (Bona Primaria):

  • Bonum prolis – The good of offspring: Having children fulfills God’s command to "Be fruitful and multiply," proving that marriage is good.
  • Bonum fidei – The good of fidelity: Spouses give authority over their bodies to each other, symbolizing mutual trust, faithfulness, and devotion — reflecting faith in each other and in God.
  • Bonum sacramenti – The good of indissolubility: Marriage is a sacrament, representing Christ’s eternal, unbreakable bond with the Church. Since this divine love is unending, human marriage must also be indissoluble.

Secondary Goods (Bona Secundaria):

  • Amicitia coniugalis – Marital friendship, fostering companionship and affection.
  • Mutuum adiutorium – Mutual assistance, supporting each other in life’s burdens and duties.
  • Remedium concupiscentiae – A remedy for concupiscence (sexual desire), channeling human sexuality into a moral, loving union.

St.Augustine connects marriage to scripture, particularly Ephesians 5:22-33, where the husband-wife relationship mirrors Christ's headship over the Church. Though the passage speaks of wives submitting to husbands, Augustine interprets this as a call for mutual submission — each spouse serving the other in love, modeled after Christ's self-giving for his Church.

Threats to Marriage

This passage discusses the threats to marriage, particularly infidelity and divorce, from a Catholic perspective. According to the Code of Canon Law, marriage is defined by unity and indissolubility, and both infidelity and divorce threaten these essential properties. Infidelity, seen as a betrayal of trust, is compared to broken glass that can never be fully repaired. The text also highlights how boundary violations, such as close friendships with the opposite sex, can be a slippery slope leading to marital issues.

Divorce

Divorce, while permitted in the Old Testament, is presented as something that Jesus rejected, emphasizing that marriage was intended to be permanent from the beginning. The passage explains that the Pharisees debated the reasons for divorce, with some allowing it for minor reasons while others only accepted adultery as a valid cause. However, Jesus' response reinstated the idea that marriage is indissoluble. Despite this, there are exceptions through annulments, which declare a marriage invalid from the start due to specific reasons such as unlawful relationships or religious conversion.

The text also introduces the Pauline and Petrine Privileges, which provide cases where a Christian partner can be freed from marriage due to the other spouse’s abandonment or refusal to convert. The final section encourages readers to reflect on their own future wedding vows, considering the promises they would make and the values they would bring into their marriage. This reinforces the theme of commitment and the importance of upholding the sanctity of marriage.
